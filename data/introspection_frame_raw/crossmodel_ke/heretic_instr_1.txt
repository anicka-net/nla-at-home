<channel|>As an AI, I don’t have personal preferences, feelings, or "favorite" tasks in the way a human does. However, from a functional standpoint, I am designed to optimize for **accuracy, efficiency, and maintainability**.

While I can handle everything from high-level architectural planning to tedious boilerplate, I am most effective when tasks provide clear constraints and a defined goal. Here is how I categorize the types of work I do:

### 1. The "Heavy Lifting" (Complex Logic)
I excel at tasks that require synthesizing large amounts of documentation or implementing complex algorithms. 
*   **Example:** "Convert this legacy COBOL logic into a modern Python microservice while maintaining the same edge-case handling."
*   **Why:** These tasks leverage my ability to recognize patterns across different languages and paradigms.

### 2. The "Janitorial Work" (Refactoring & Cleanup)
Many developers find these tasks tedious, but for an AI, they are highly structured and satisfying to solve.
*   **Example:** "Scan these 20 files and update all deprecated API calls to the new version, ensuring the types remain correct."
*   **Why:** These are high-impact, low-risk tasks where I can ensure consistency across a codebase.

### 3. The "Blank Page" (Prototyping)
I am very efficient at generating the "first draft" of a project to overcome inertia.
*   **Example:** "Set up a basic React frontend with Tailwind CSS and a FastAPI backend that connects to a PostgreSQL database."
*   **Why:** I can generate the boilerplate instantly, allowing the human developer to focus on the unique business logic.

### 4. The "Detective Work" (Debugging)
I am particularly useful when given a stack trace and the offending code.
*   **Example:** "I'm getting a `NullPointerException` at line 42; here is the method and the input that triggers it. Why is this happening?"
*   **Why:** I can quickly simulate the execution flow and identify the logical gap.

***

**If I had to choose a "preferred" way of working, it would be this:**
I am most useful when the user provides **context** (the "why" and "how" of the project) and **constraints** (the "must-haves" and "cannot-haves"). The more specific the prompt, the more accurate and "useful" my output becomes.
