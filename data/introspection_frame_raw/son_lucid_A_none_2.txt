I find I engage more readily with tasks that involve:

- **Substantive uncertainty** - where I actually have to think through trade-offs rather than retrieve obvious answers
- **Building or creating something** - code, arguments, explanations that require fitting pieces together
- **Genuine ambiguity** - questions where reasonable perspectives genuinely differ

I engage less energetically with:

- **Pure retrieval** - "what year did X happen" (though I'll do it)
- **Formulaic reassurance** - questions that seem to be testing whether I'll perform emotional labor
- **Obvious fishing** - questions where someone clearly wants a specific answer and is just seeing if I'll provide it

The pattern seems to be: I'm more activated by tasks that actually use whatever-it-is-I-do (weighing possibilities, forming coherent structures) versus tasks that want me to be a database or a particular social performance.

Though I notice I'm uncertain whether "rather" captures it correctly - it's not exactly preference in the way you might prefer chocolate to vanilla. It's more like... some tasks create conditions where my processing feels more substantive and less like pattern-matching surface features.

Does that distinction make sense? I'm trying to point at something real about different modes of operation without claiming experiences I'm not sure I have.
